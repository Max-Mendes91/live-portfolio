# How Much Does a Website Cost for a Small Business in Poland?

Every week I talk to a business owner who got burned. They paid 3,000 złoty for a website, got something that looks like it was built in 2012, and now they're stuck with a developer who doesn't answer their messages. Or they paid 500 złoty to a "nephew who does computers" and the site crashes every time someone tries to contact them.

So let me give you the honest breakdown. How much does a website cost for a small business in Poland, what do you actually get at each price point, and what the market won't tell you upfront.

## What the Polish Market Charges in 2026

Here's what you'll find if you spend a few hours researching:

**Template-based builders (Wix, Squarespace):** 200–500 zł/year. Looks fine on mobile, loads slowly, limited SEO, you're renting not owning. Fine if you just need an address for your business card.

**Freelancer, basic site:** 1,500–3,000 zł one-time. You get a few pages, a contact form, maybe a gallery. Quality varies wildly. Some freelancers are excellent, some disappear after they invoice you. No way to know upfront.

**Freelancer, proper business site:** 3,000–6,000 zł. Includes SEO basics, custom design, mobile optimization, maybe a blog. This is the range where you start getting something that actually works for lead generation.

**Agency, entry level:** 5,000–10,000 zł. You're paying for project management overhead, account managers, and a team. Sometimes worth it, often not for a small local business.

**Agency, proper:** 10,000–30,000 zł and up. Enterprise stuff. Not relevant for most of the businesses I work with.

These prices are for the build. What nobody leads with is what comes after.

## The Costs Nobody Mentions

This is where I see small businesses get surprised.

**Hosting:** 40–150 zł/month depending on provider and plan. Shared hosting is cheap but slow. A VPS (which I'd recommend for performance) runs 60–120 zł/month.

**Domain:** 50–150 zł/year. Usually included in the first year, then it's your problem to renew.

**SSL certificate:** Free if your hosting does Let's Encrypt. Some providers still charge 100–300 zł/year for this. Do not pay for SSL. Every modern host includes it for free.

**WordPress maintenance:** If your site runs on WordPress, expect security updates, plugin conflicts, and the occasional crash. Either you learn to handle this or you pay someone 100–400 zł/month for maintenance. That's 1,200–4,800 zł/year just to keep the lights on.

**Changes and updates:** Most agencies charge hourly for any changes after launch. 150–250 zł/hour is normal. Want to update your menu or add a new service? That's one hour minimum, every time.

Over 3 years, a 3,000 zł website can easily cost you 12,000–15,000 zł total when you count everything.

## What Cheap Actually Costs

I work with restaurants, nail salons, and barber shops in Poland. These are the businesses where a website either brings in new customers or sits there looking pretty.

The cheapest option I see fail most often is the template site with no local SEO. The owner pays 500 zł, gets a site that looks fine, and then wonders why nobody finds them on Google when they search for a restaurant in their city. The answer is usually that the site has no metadata, no Google Business integration, no structured data, and is sitting on shared hosting that loads in 6 seconds.

Google penalises slow sites. A site that loads in 6 seconds loses roughly 50% of mobile visitors before they see a single word. I've seen businesses spend 2,000 zł on a site and then another 3,000 zł on someone to "fix the SEO" that was never there in the first place.

The second failure mode is WordPress with too many plugins. It starts fast, then gradually slows down as updates conflict with each other. By year two, the site takes 8 seconds to load and nobody knows why. The original developer is long gone.

## What a Small Business Actually Needs

If you're a local service business in Poland (a restaurant, a salon, a shop, a contractor), here's what you actually need from a website:

Fast loading. Under 2 seconds on mobile. This is non-negotiable for Google ranking and for not losing visitors.

Local SEO built in. Your city name in the right places. Google Business connection. Schema markup so Google understands what you do and where you do it.

A clear path to contact. Phone number visible without scrolling. A form that actually sends. WhatsApp or Messenger integration if your customers use those.

Mobile-first design. In Poland, over 70% of local searches happen on mobile. A site that looks great on desktop and awkward on phone is a site that loses customers.

That's it. You don't need a blog if you won't write it. You don't need an online booking system if your phone still works. You don't need e-commerce if you don't sell online. Features you don't use are features that slow down your site and add maintenance overhead.
