# The Dead Internet Is Here — And That's Why a Human-Made Website Matters More Than Ever

I scroll through a client's Google Maps reviews and something feels off. The language is too clean. The phrasing is identical across three different profiles. The accounts are two weeks old with no photo, no history, no other activity. Classic bot behavior. But this time it's not just reviews. It's the content on their competitors' websites too.

That moment stuck with me. Because it pointed at something I'd been noticing for months: the internet is filling up with content that wasn't written by anyone.

## What the Dead Internet Theory Actually Got Right

The dead internet theory has been around since at least 2021. The original idea was conspiratorial: that governments and corporations had secretly replaced most online activity with bots and AI to manufacture consensus. That part was always hard to verify.

But the core observation, that the internet feels less human than it used to, turned out to be accurate in a way the theory's authors didn't fully predict.

According to the [2025 Imperva Bad Bot Report](https://www.imperva.com/blog/2025-imperva-bad-bot-report-how-ai-is-supercharging-the-bot-threat/), automated traffic accounted for 51% of all internet traffic in 2024. For the first time in a decade, bots outnumber humans online. Not slightly. Bots are the majority.

And that's just traffic. The content side is arguably worse.

## The AI Slop Flood Is Already Here

In March 2026, a game called "Your AI Slop Bores Me" went viral on Hacker News. The premise: instead of trying to detect AI, players pretend to be AI and generate the most generic, soulless content they can. The joke landed because everyone immediately got it. We've all been on the receiving end of this content. Blog posts that answer questions nobody asked. About pages that describe a company as "passionate about delivering innovative solutions." LinkedIn posts that exist only to exist.

The game worked as satire because the reality it was mocking is already mainstream.

Reddit threads are full of bots commenting on posts. LinkedIn has profiles that generate 10 posts a day and never interact with anyone. GitHub has accounts that make meaningless commits to appear active. The pattern is everywhere. Fake engagement. Fake content. Fake presence.

BBC's More or Less programme covered this in early 2026, asking "How dead is the internet?" The conclusion wasn't doom. But it confirmed what most people using the web already feel: you have to work harder to find something real.

## Why AI-Generated Websites Are Starting to Fail

Here's the thing about AI content floods: they don't just affect the content. They affect trust.

When a potential customer lands on a business website in 2026, they're already pattern-matching for authenticity. They've been burned before. A site that reads like it was generated in 30 seconds, with stock photos and generic paragraphs about "quality" and "experience," doesn't make them feel confident. It makes them click back.

Google is adjusting too. Its E-E-A-T framework (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) has been the ranking standard for years. But the emphasis on the first E, actual experience, is growing stronger as AI content floods search results. A study by [Rankability](https://www.rankability.com/data/does-google-penalize-ai-content/) found that 83% of top-ranking results use human-generated content. Google doesn't want to serve AI summaries of AI summaries. It wants sources.

For local businesses, this is even more direct. A nail salon in Częstochowa asking "how did you find us?" and getting "Google search" means Google trusted that website. And Google trusted it because it had real photos, real reviews, real language, real local signals. You can't fake that at scale. A bot can't write about what it's like to park on Śląska Street at 7pm on a Tuesday.

## Why Authentic Local Content Is a Moat Right Now

I build [websites for Polish SMBs](/en/services/web-development). Restaurants, nail salons, barbers, service businesses. Most of them have no website, or one that was built in 2015 and hasn't been touched since.

The window I'm seeing right now is specific. Most AI-generated websites sound the same. Same structure, same vocabulary, same promises. They're built fast and they look like it. Meanwhile, a local business in a Polish city has an inherently unique story: the owner's name, the neighborhood, the services that locals actually ask for, the photos from inside the shop.

That specificity is the moat. It can't be generated. It has to be gathered.

When I write copy for a client's site, I ask questions. What do your regulars say about you? What do you do differently from the place two streets over? What's the neighborhood like? Those answers don't exist in a training dataset. They exist in a conversation.

A [human-made website](/en/services/web-development) built on those answers will outrank, out-convert, and out-trust a generated one. Not because it's pretty. Because it's true.

## The Opportunity in the Noise

The AI slop flood is a problem. But it's also a gap.

When 90% of new business websites are built with AI tools and look identical, the 10% that are clearly made by a real person for a real business stand out immediately. Customers notice. Search engines notice.

I'm not against using AI in a workflow. I use it constantly. But there's a difference between using AI to help build something human, and using AI to replace the human entirely. The first can produce a great website. The second produces something that blends into the noise.

The businesses I work with don't need to win the internet. They need to win their city. A bakery in Gliwice doesn't need to rank globally. It needs the person two kilometers away who just searched for a birthday cake to find it, trust it, and call.

That's a very solvable problem. But you need a real website to solve it.

I'll keep writing about this as the AI content landscape evolves. The patterns are just starting to settle.
